2013 Tour de France

  • Bike
    Surly Long-Haul Trucker

    I purchased the 700C wheel version as a complete from a local bike shop and it proved to be a bomb-proof bike. The only time I had to replace anything on it was after a crash.
  • Upgrades
    Retroshift CX2 Shifters

    Spending most of my riding time up on the hoods, these kept me from having to reach for the bar ends.
  • Shimano PD-M536 SPD Pedals

    You either love them or hate them (clip-in pedals). I'm in the love camp. These came off my 7+ year old mountain bike, but performed with absolutely no issues.
  • Schwalbe Marathon Plus Tires (35mm)

    Not a single flat in 6643 km! Enough said.
  • Selle Royal Respiro Soft Athletic Saddle

    Nothing too special, but provided a little extra ischial cushioning for the slightly more upright riding position of the touring bike geometry.
  • Accessories
    SKS Bluemels Mudguards

    The fit of these mudguards/fenders is superb with absolutely no rubbing or rattling. These definitely made wet days less miserable!
  • Old Man Mountain Cold Springs Rear Rack

    Also borrowed from my mountain bike, an extremely tough, axle-mounted rack for carrying heavier panniers. No complaints here.
  • Arkel AC LowRider Front Rack

    Tough rack with a low center of gravity. Got bent slightly off center after a crash, but was easily bent back without structural damage.
  • Cable Lock

    Not the most secure option for locking a bike, but relatively lightweight and very versatile. I was rarely more than 100m away from my bike for the whole trip, so I felt secure enough with this type of lock.
  • Miscellaneous

    Double-wrapped bar tape helped with road vibration.
    Bar-end mounted mirror was useful, although constantly required re-adjusting.
  • Tools
    Hex Driver/Chain Tool Multitool

    The multi-tool was essential for making adjustments (to nearly every component of the bike over the course of the trip) although I never had need of the chain tool (never broke the chain).
  • Pedal Wrench

    Taking a pedal wrench was overkill. It was useful when taking the bike apart to fly it home, but being a fairly heavy item, ultimately not something I would take with me again. I suppose I could have clubbed a bear with it if the need/opportunity arose.
  • Zip-Ties

    Yes - they do deserve their own row . I took about 50 medium-sized ties and somehow managed to use all of them. Most of them were used to hold together my fragile panniers.
  • Pump

    I went through three pumps. The first one I lost, the second one (from Canadian Tire) broke on its second use. The third is the best I've owned - with a hose and pressure guage. No more hand-pumps for me!
  • Gerber Swagger 20cm folding Knife

    Useful for cutting things .
  • Parts
    Spare Tube x2
    Spare Tire x1
    Tire Patch Kit x5
    Spare Brake Cable x2
    Spare Shifter Cable
    Spare Spoke x2
    Spare SPD Pedal Cleat x2

    I grouped these all together because they all share something in common: I never used any of them! Why five stars? I slept better knowing I had them all with me. SPD Cleats really don't wear out quickly though - I probably should have left those out.
  • Cooking
    MSR XGK-EX Multi-Fuel Stove
    MSR Fuel Bottle

    I was so excited for this piece of gear - a liquid fuel stove with a reusable fuel bottle that can run off of unleaded gasoline! I used it so infrequently (~15-20 meals) that I never used up the 650ml of white gas I filled it with before I left. If I did it again, I would just take a small solid fuel stove. Definitely over-powered for cooking, but not impossible to use. Sounds like a jet!
  • MSR Alpine 2 Pot Set

    A nice lightweight pot/pan set with a detachable handle that fit my stove inside for storage. I only took the larger of the two included pots with me.
  • Hydration
    Generic 750ml Bottle x2

    Filled these up wherever possible and added hydration mix on hot days.
  • 1L Platypus PlusBottle x3

    These foldable bottles were perfect for carrying extra water on long days and in the heat. When empty, they take up very little space and are lightweight. No leaks or complaints.
  • Shelter
    Sierra Designs Tent (2 person)

    Graciously lent to me, this tent was an ideal size, allowing storage of most of my gear inside the tent at night. I did a fresh seam seal on it before leaving, and it kept water out quite well except in utter downpours.
  • Dollar-Store Tarp

    Probably the best $2.50 spent on gear. In the downpours, this helped keep the inside of the tent drier (not completely dry, but what can you really expect for $2.50?).
  • Sleeping
    Thermarest Z-Lite SOL Sleeping Pad

    Not exactly plush, but super compact, lightweight and the thermal side kept me comfortable on the nights the temperature crept below 0 C.
  • Down Sleeping Bag

    A family heirloom, this lightweight down bag turned out to be perfect for the task. Due to its age, I don't have any specs on it, but I'm glad I didn't take anything warmer or heavier! I can't say I was always warm at night, but I was never so cold that I couldn't sleep.
  • Luggage
    Axiom Grand Tour 45 & 30 Panniers

    These panniers were quite ightweight, provided many different-sized pockets, and were relateively inexpensive. Capacity-wise they were great as well, I wouldn't have wanted any more space to fill. At ~70lbs loaded, more space would not have been a good idea! I expected them to be more durable however. All of the small modular outer bags eventually tore away from the main bag, leaving holes that destroyed the water-proofing. Zip-ties held them together but I guess I was expecting to be able to use them again after this trip. Maybe I was expecting too much.
  • Arkel Small Handlebar Bag

    This bag was useful on many levels. I kept valuables (money & tech) in it, so when I locked up the bike when going into stores or at other stops, I could unclip it and use the shoulder strap to take it with me. Not strictly waterproof, but the water-resistant liner was enough to keep my tech inside safe from water damage.
  • Wearables
    Lake MX-165 SPD Cycling Shoes
    Lightweight Running Shoes
    Flip-Flops

    I was surprised at how many times I had to walk my bike up hills - sometimes due to grade, other times due to heavy traffic and shoulder conditions. I was glad that my cycling shoes were very walkable in these cases. The running shoes were great for days off were I wanted to walk in a little more comfort and flip-flops were great for walking around campsites at the end of the day.
  • Gore Windstopper Base Layer
    Icebreaker Wool Tee

    I'll spare you the details of every piece of clothing I took, but these two items were two of my favourites (both gifts - thanks again!). The base layer was so insulating on cold and windy days that I could get away without wearing my jacket. One item I forgot to pack was my warm cycling gloves. I missed these a lot on cold mornings (of which there were many!).
  • Waterproof Jacket
    Waterproof Pants
    Helmet Cover
    Shoe Covers

    Ultimately not the most useful for longer-distance riding. I found that at most, rain gear would keep me dry for about 1 hour in moderate rain. Given that I averaged about 8-10 hours of riding a day, it was only really useful for short rainshowers. If it was raining heavily or for long periods, there wasn't much I could do to stay dry.
  • Ordnance
    Frontiersman Bear Spray

    Lent to me from a fellow-adventurer, this was nice to have and nice to never need.
  • Bug Spray (with Deet)

    The bugs will still swarm, but less of them will touch down with this stuff all over you.
  • Misc.
    Mini First Aid Kit

    The stars of this kit were the Ibuprofen (anti-inflammatory for muscle and joint pain - mainly after one crash) and Chlor-tripolon (antihistamine for particularly bad bug bites). Thankfully I never really needed the blood-and-gore parts of the kit.
  • Sunscreen

    I got lazy with it a few times near the begining and regretted it. I got some heavier SPF sunscreen for my face and ears after realizing that it kept getting burnt no matter how much I sprayed it. I also took a bunch of Eco-Lips SPF lip balm which was great at preventing wind and sun burn.
  • Chamois Cream

    I didn't use it all that much, but it kept things comfy on a number of occasions. I'll leave it at that.
  • Electronics
    iPhone

    This was my favourite piece of tech over the trip. More below on apps that I found useful.
  • iPod Nano

    Nice to have for the days that weren't too windy and to liven up the monotonous days.
  • Sony NEX-F3 Compact System Camera

    Lightweight 16MP camera that created some great images to work with. With a 64GB SDXC card in it, I didn't have to carry extra memory even shooting at 16MP.
  • Powertraveller Power Monkey Extreme 9000mAh Solar Charger

    Another piece of gear I was really excited for. Unfortunately the solar panel failed for no apparent reason in the first week. The battery was still useful for charging my phone and Mp3 player (2 full phone charges in one discharge of the battery), but I had to find power outlets to charge it from.
  • Cateye Strada Double Wireless Computer

    Just a basic cycling computer with speed and distance metrics. The CR-2032 sensor battery died in it after a month, and I didn't bother replacing it.
  • Light and Motion Urban 400 Headlight
    Battery-powered Tail Light

    Both borrowed from my commuter bike, I found I rarely needed these lights as I was never riding in the dark. On foggy and rainy days they were good to have though.
  • Apps
    TrackMyTour

    This iPhone app was incredible. It allowed me to share GPS waypoints with anyone who had a link to my map and to stay in touch over the course of the trip. Even without 3G or WIFI, I could save waypoints offline for upload later. TrackMyTour even provides a .kml file download of all your waypoints after a trip, which I used to create the map at the top of this page. Check out my TrackMyTour map here.
  • RideWithGPS

    A web app that allowed me to create gpx files (GPS-tagged routes) of each day's route plan. Using these files, I could plan each day's distance and download the appropriate map tiles for offline viewing while on the road (using MOBAC below).
  • Mobile Atlas Creator (MOBAC)

    An app for OSX that allowed me to download map tiles for storage on my phone in order to be able to view and zoom maps of my route even when offline (in areas without 3G cell service).
  • Galileo Offline Maps

    An iPhone app that allowed me to transfer, manage and view all the offline maps I downloaded using MOBAC and RideWithGPS on my phone.
